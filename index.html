Description


What makes Art so interesting, sometimes overwhelming, shocking, beautiful or surprising. What is happening in our brains when watching, hearing, or feeling art? What makes art Art? Is it possible to scientifically study such questions?

In recent years there is a growing interest in connecting art and science. There are more and more scientific studies on art and science and even international conferences on this topic. Cognitive Neuroscientists, Experimental Psychologists, Designers and various other disciplines can learn a lot from art, and the way artists work, and artist may benefit from insights in these fields of science.

On Friday 29th, members of Donders Institute for Brain Cognition and Behaviour and the Valkhof Museum organize a symposium on this topic. The entrance is free but registration is mandatory. Please only register if you intend to join the symposium as the number of seats is limited.

Claus-Christian Carbon

Title: Old Masters, new insights: About the perceptual expertise of great artists

Abstract: To view artworks in museums can be interesting in many ways: we can enjoy the mere looking at the works, we can learn about artistic styles and about new facts around the artist, the production and the art market. To inspect artworks, however, can also be very insightful to understand perception, as many artists were real experts on several perceptual phenomena—some of them devoted a good part of their life to conduct scientific studies on perception. I will refer to two very prominent but different cases, one being Leonardo from Vinci: one of the most influential Renaissance artists—and the other being Johannes Vermeer from Delft: one of the great masters of Baroque time. The talk develops a more differentiated picture on these Old Masters than often found in uncritical public depictions and popular books where they are bluntly praised and beyond all measures characterized as geniuses. I will focus on some major achievements of their work to make clear that there is good reason to call them great masters of perceptual science, providing a great opportunity of fascinating people for art and science while experiencing pleasure when viewing their works.

Johan Wagemans

Title: Perceptual organization in vision and visual art

Abstract: Perceptual organization constitutes a key topic of research in vision science since the early days of Gestalt psychology, over a century ago. It is also fundamental to the experience of visual art. A good deal of what makes an art work visually and aesthetically appealing lies in the way the parts can be integrated into a larger whole. The 'beholder's share' describes the flexible organization of the parts into a coherent Gestalt, a process whose result is the viewer's pleasure and fascination. My talk will explore this central theme, focusing on some empirical studies into pictorial relief and shape (e.g., in Picasso's sketches of female nudes) and some collaborative projects with contemporary artists (e.g., Anne-Mie Van Kerckhoven).

Johan Wagemans

Professor, University of Leuven, Belgium

Johan Wagemans, has a BA in psychology and philosophy, an MSc and a PhD in experimental psychology, all from the University of Leuven, where he is currently a full professor. Current research interests are mainly in so-called mid-level vision (perceptual grouping, figure-ground organization, depth and shape perception) but stretching out to low-level vision (contrast detection and discrimination) and high-level vision (object recognition and categorization), including applications in autism, arts and sports (see www.gestaltrevision.be). He has recently edited the Oxford Handbook of Perceptual Organization.

Claus-Christian Carbon

Professor, University of Bamberg, Germany

Claus-Christian Carbon studied Psychology (Dipl.-Psych.), followed by Philosophy (M.A.), received his PhD from FU Berlin and made his "Habilitation" at University of Vienna. He is a full professor leading the Department of General Psychology and Methodology (U Bamberg) and EPÆG—a research group devoted to enhancing the knowledge and methodology in the fields of cognitive ergonomics, psychological aesthetics and Gestalt. He is an editor of several scientific journals, among them Art & Perception as Editor-of-Chief. CCC is head of the Bamberg Graduate School of Cognitive and Affective Sciences (BaGrACS) which he established in 2013.

Laurien Renckens

Artist, Nijmegen

Dutch artist Laurien Renckens, based in the Netherlands, experienced an enormous development in her work. During that period she constructed, scraped off, started new and painted layer over layer. “My work is now getting transparent more and more. It is a mission to emptiness in which I experience the perfection”. She explores to touch the essence with the fewest possible means, no noise, absolute in the gesture of brush and color, straight in the moment.

Frank ter Beek

Artist, Nijmegen

In a playground full of open-ended experiments with materials, objects and shapes I create sculptures, video, performance, photographs and installations. Humorous, a bit absurd, yet sensual and tactile. Sometimes the material still literally displays my fingerprint. My actions consist of assembling, combining, building and performing. I consider everything to be useful material in that process: lead, wax, water, air, video pixels, printer ink and even my own body.

Rob van Lier

Associate Professor, Radboud University

Rob van Lier studied Experimental Psychology, and after that he took his PhD on the topic of human visual perception at Radboud University (Nijmegen, the Netherlands). Next, he spent a few postdoc years at the University of Leuven (Belgium) and returned to Nijmegen, based on a two-year grant from the Dutch National Science Foundation (NWO) and a 5-year grant from the Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences (KNAW). Currently, he is an associate professor at the Radboud University and a principal investigator at the Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition and Behaviour (Nijmegen, The Netherlands), heading the research group 'Perception and Awareness'.
